
- most obvious, and also most important, argument is that gfa managed to finally end the decade-long violence of the troubles

- most paramilitary groups declared ceasefires and started decommissioning weapons

- because of inclusion of paramilitaries in negotiations, reignition of violence was highly unlikely

- intercommunal violence decreased rapidly, not only in cities but also in rural areas 

- as result of this daily life became much safer for the general population, especially for young people growing up after 1998

- because of this, agreement was generally recognized as positive by the "normal" people -> 71% in NI and 94% in RoI

---

- the second big strength was of gfa, it provided a slow, but stable and working political system for the 28 years after the agreement

- for the first time in a long time NI had a local, from the uk devolved government, in which both unionists and nationalist are pretty much equally represented

- other new institutions (NI Assembly, North/South Council, British-Irish Council) also further strengthened british-irish cooperation and mutual trust

- stability, which almost everyone agrees the gfa provided, was ranked as #1 priority by most people in NI in a 2023 poll conducted by the ohio state university, moreso than quick political progress

- danger of escalating violence if agreement ever were to fail, probably enough to keep up cooperation between ireland and the united kingdom, further guaranteeing peace and stability
